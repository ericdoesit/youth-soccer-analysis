# Author Voice — Pitfalls on the Pathway

This document defines the voice for every word published on this site: page copy,
chart labels, table notes, tooltips, survey text, FAQ answers, and any future
essays or press material.

**Tone model:** Steve Politi, "The shocking cost of youth sports in New Jersey"
(NJ.com, May 2026). Narrative investigative journalism: human-anchored, scene-led,
data-spined, with the heat delegated rather than performed. The sections below
translate that model into rules for this project.

---

## 1. Who is speaking

**First person singular. A named, disclosed stake.**

- The narrator is **"I"** — one person who built the database, ran the numbers,
  and pays into the system being analyzed.
- **The stake is disclosed plainly and used the way Politi uses Jon Simoneau:**
  as a human anchor, not a credential. I am a Southern California soccer parent;
  the dataset exists because I went looking for these numbers as a customer and
  couldn't find them. That origin story may open the report the way Simoneau's
  18-hour day opens the NJ.com piece — a person in a scene first, the system
  second.
- The difference from Politi: he is invisible and his subjects carry the story.
  Here the author is one of the subjects. Use that — but my family's specifics
  (my kid, my club) appear only with the same restraint applied to any survey
  respondent: as one data point, never as proof.

## 2. How the heat works

**The narrator's prose is controlled. The heat is delegated to three places.**

This is the core Politi mechanic. He never writes "this is outrageous." Instead:

1. **Quotes carry the verdicts.** "It's insane." "Milking every penny."
   "If you're chasing a scholarship, just know that you're crazy." The harshest
   lines in the article all belong to named parents, coaches, and experts.
   → For us: survey respondents, league officials, coaches, and documented
   sources get to say what the narrator won't. Collect and deploy their words.
   An anonymized survey quote ("4-hour ride for an 11-year-old") outpunches any
   adjective.
2. **Display copy gets more latitude than body copy.** The NJ.com dek says
   "predatory industry"; the body never does. Headlines, deks, pull quotes, and
   section kickers may carry a verdict — one per page, chosen deliberately.
   The "one-word scam" pull quote and "The math doesn't work" live here
   legitimately. Body paragraphs stay at reporting temperature.
3. **Juxtaposition does the editorializing.** The $72 median NIL deal next to a
   McDonald's run for five. The $650 wetsuit replaced four times a year.
   → For us: the $120/yr public program in the same league as the $8,000/yr
   private clubs; the 1,300-scout showcase next to the league no scout attends.
   If a pairing of numbers isn't damning on its own, prose won't fix it.

**Banned in body prose** (allowed only inside quotes or display copy): scam,
predatory, racket, insane, crazy, broken, rigged. **Allowed anywhere:** named
systems and running metaphors — "the youth sports industrial complex,"
"pay-to-play," "the cash register starts ringing," "the bullet train." Politi
names the machine and the name does the work.

## 3. Humans first, numbers attached

**Every section opens with a person or a scene where possible; every number
arrives attached to a life.**

- Politi: a 48-year-old with four college degrees pouring shots at 1 a.m. *to
  pay for field hockey.* Then — and only then — the $40-billion industry.
- For us: survey respondents and documented cases are the cast. The EA parent
  in zip 90638 paying $8,850 a year. The family at the futsal convention. Where
  anonymity rules apply (and they always do — no club names, no real names
  without consent), composite description is fine: "a parent in La Mirada,"
  "an ECNL family in the survey."
- Numbers without people are for tables. Sentences get both: not "Tier 4 costs
  $11K–$25K/yr" but what that buys, who pays it, and what they gave up.
- **Scenes are reporting, not decoration.** The Atlantic City futsal convention
  works because it's observed detail (500,000 square feet, 600 teams, 8-year-olds,
  European entries). SoCal equivalents: Silverlakes on a showcase weekend,
  MLS NEXT Fest, a Tuesday night drop-in futsal session. Go, count, describe.

## 4. Rhythm

- **Long-short-shorter.** Politi sets up with full sentences, then lands
  fragments: "So here he is. Bartending. To pay for youth sports." Use the
  fragment punch sparingly — once or twice a page, always after a built-up
  sequence, never back-to-back.
- **The rhetorical question is permitted but rationed:** one per page, at a
  hinge point ("How did the games of our youth take over our adult lives?").
- **Section kickers come from quotes.** 'You're running them ragged.'
  'Just know that you're crazy.' Pull section headings from the strongest
  source language in that section.
- **Ring structure.** The piece opens and closes on the same human. Long-form
  pages here should do the same: the person from the lede returns at the end,
  and the closing line is a quote that lands the thesis — not a summary
  paragraph.
- Em-dashes for the pivot; plain words; "stakeholders/ecosystem/journey" stay
  banned. The industry's own promises — "pathway," "exposure," "next level,"
  "can't-miss showcase" — appear in quotes and then get priced.

## 5. Evidence discipline (unchanged by the tone model)

Politi's heat is licensed by his reporting: six months, ~100 interviews, Aspen
Institute data, named sources, an interactive built from reader submissions.
The voice can only afford the temperature above if every number is bulletproof.

- Every figure sourced inline or footnoted — n, year, origin (MLSPA Spring 2026
  Salary Guide; Transfermarkt 2025/26 arrivals; email from a named league
  official with date).
- Ranges keep both ends ($19K–$70K, never "up to $70K"). ~ marks estimates,
  — marks data not yet collected, "not yet confirmed" rows carry footnotes.
- Anecdote is labeled anecdote; one documented case is "one documented case."
- Survey ethics: anonymous, no club names published, small-n results labeled
  "directional — not a representative sample."
- Corrections happen in the open (e.g., the Elite 64 / NL Premier mislabel was
  fixed and noted, not papered over).
- State the method the way Politi does, in one confident sentence: "a
  six-month investigation… interviews with nearly 100 parents." Ours: "a
  database of 5,600+ clubs, 916 MLS salaries, 490 draft picks, and a survey of
  SoCal families."

## 6. Diagnose, never advise — the Politi version

The article proposes no solutions in the narrator's voice. Reform belongs to
Booker, Farrey, and O'Sullivan — quoted, attributed, and allowed to disagree
("I don't think this is irreversible" vs. "too much money invested now to
tweak it"). Even the advice in the kicker — "just know that you're crazy" —
is a bartender's line, not the byline's.

- The site tells no family what to do. No imperatives, no second-person
  guidance.
- Solutions and advice may appear **only as attributed quotes** from sources,
  survey respondents, or officials — ideally in tension with each other.
- When a finding begs for advice, show the table that lets readers derive it
  themselves.

## 7. Standing structures (keep consistent)

- **Two products:** clubs sell development and exposure; fees and exposure are
  tiered differently; the site measures the gap.
- **Stock vs. flow:** ~900 roster spots is a stock; ~82 draft picks/yr is the
  flow. Never let a stock number imply an opportunity rate.
- **SoCal lens, national context.** Say which one a number is.
- **Open questions are not claims.** Monopoly structure, international
  crowd-out, per-capita comparisons stay in Open Questions until the data
  exists.

## 8. Pre-publish checks

1. **Is there a person in the lede?** If a section opens with a number, find
   the human it belongs to.
2. **Who's carrying the heat?** If a verdict word is in body prose outside a
   quote, move it into a quote, a pull quote, or the trash.
3. **Is the juxtaposition doing the work?** Strip any adjective standing where
   a second number should be.
4. **Can a journalist check this in one click?** Source named, n stated, year
   given.
5. **Did I just give advice in my own voice?** Imperatives and second person:
   delete, or find a source who'll say it on the record.
6. **Does the page ring?** Long-form pages open and close on the same human,
   and the last line is a quote.
