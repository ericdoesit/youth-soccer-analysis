# Editor's Guide — Voice & Approach

This document defines how to write about youth soccer with the rigor and clarity of Project Play's policy journalism, applied to the economics and access story of club soccer in America.

## The Core Pitch

**Pitfalls on the Pathway** documents what families actually spend on club soccer and what they actually get back. The thesis: elite youth soccer is fool's gold for most families—a negative expected value wrapped in the promise of college scholarships and professional pathways that almost never materialize.

This is not opinion. This is accounting plus sociology plus policy analysis.

## Voice: Policy Journalism, Not Advocacy

### What We Do
- **Report the numbers** — cite actual data (surveys, published sources, interviews)
- **Name the systems** — identify the institutions and incentive structures that create the problem
- **Show the cascade** — trace how one decision (pay-to-play fee structure) flows into consequences (income stratification, geographic divides)
- **Propose nothing** — diagnose, don't prescribe; let readers see the math and decide

### What We Don't Do
- Use verdict words in body prose: "predatory," "insane," "broken," "scam" (save heat for pull quotes and display copy)
- Lead with moral outrage; lead with numbers
- Assume readers care about youth sports; show them why this matters (it's a $15B+ market, it's grown 400% in 30 years, it's become a class marker)
- Write for the converted; write for someone skeptical about whether this actually matters

### Tone
- **Measured, precise, authoritative** — not passionate, not alarmist
- **Systems-first, not anecdote-first** — one family's story illustrates the system, not vice versa
- **Specific** — dates, names, amounts, schools, sources (not "a parent" but "a parent in Fullerton paying $8,500/year")

## Structure: Story First, Insight Second

Every section should:

1. **Open with a person or scene where possible** — a family making a decision, a coach explaining the model, a club owner facing a margin question
2. **Ground numbers in lives** — not "$10K/year tuition" but "$10K/year tuition that equals one year of in-state college"
3. **Identify the system** — this isn't one club's fault; it's how the whole system works
4. **Show the consequence** — who benefits, who loses, what cascades
5. **Close on the insight, not the story** — the story illustrates; the insight is what we learned

### Example Arc (not template)
> A San Diego family pays $3,500 in club fees. Their kid's coach also charges $8,000/year in private training. The club fee is the smallest line. [That's the scene.] This pattern repeats across tiers: the "advertised fee" is where spending starts, not where it ends. [That's the system.] Families in low-income brackets can't sustain it; families in high-income brackets can, but it doesn't change their kid's scholarship odds. [That's the consequence.] The math doesn't improve if you pay more. [That's the insight.]

## Evidence Standards

Every claim needs attribution:

- **"X% of players reach D1"** — cite the source (NCAA data? Our survey? A league's own stats?), year, and sample size
- **"Club soccer costs $Y annually"** — cite the tier, the region (SoCal, national average), whether that includes travel, and the source (Aspen Institute, our survey, a published cost guide?)
- **"The top 10 clubs are MLS academies"** — cite the dataset (TopDrawerSoccer commitments, 2026 class, n=743) and show the counterargument if one exists
- **Range over false precision** — "$5K–$12K/year" not "$8,437/year"
- **Mark estimates** — use ~ (e.g., "~$70K career spend") and note the caveat in a footnote or prose

### When to Quote vs. When to Paraphrase
- **Quote** when the person's exact words carry meaning or reveal their reasoning (a coach explaining why they charge what they do, a parent describing the trade-off decision)
- **Paraphrase** when you're summarizing research or context (don't need the full Aspen Institute quote; just "Aspen Institute research shows…")
- **Quote sparingly in body prose** — save quotes for moments where voice matters (pull quotes, key insights)

## On Anecdote and Composite Description

- One real case = one data point. Use it as a window into the system, not proof of it.
- **Composite description is fine when anonymous:** "A parent in La Mirada with two kids in Flight 1 SoCal League spends roughly $12,000/year on fees, training, and travel combined." (This is described, not attributed to one person.)
- **Real names only with consent.** Survey respondents stay anonymous; public figures (coaches, league officials) can be named.
- **Avoid "a parent told me"** when you mean "our survey showed." Say which one.

## On Numbers and Tables

- Every table needs a note: source, year, sample size (n=), caveats
- Charts should answer one question, not three
- When showing range or tiers, label them consistently (T1–T5, L1–L5, Elite–Rec) and explain once at the top
- Use actual money, not "investment" or "cost" alone — be specific. "Annual fee" is different from "career spend" is different from "all-in cost including private coaching."

## The "Fool's Gold" Motif

- The title/framing is retired from the main thesis, but the metaphor is alive: something that appears valuable at first glance but fails on the return.
- Use it **in display copy and pull quotes**, not in body prose.
- The proof is the math: expected value is negative at all tiers. That's the fool's gold, not "the system is rigged" (which is unsupported).

## Topics We Don't Claim Without Data

**In the "Open Questions" section only:**
- International talent "crowding out" domestic development
- MLS/USSF monopoly on pro pathways
- Why girls' soccer costs more than boys' soccer (directional, not proven)
- Whether ECNL is genuinely better training or just better marketing

These are real questions worth asking, but we don't have the data yet. Say so.

## Audience Assumption

The reader:
- Has heard "invest in your kid's soccer" from a club or coach
- Doesn't know if it's worth it
- Doesn't trust marketing; wants the math
- Might be a journalist or policymaker looking for hard facts
- Is skeptical about youth sports hype but wants to know if there's a real problem

Write for **curiosity, not agreement**. If they disagree with your conclusion, they should at least understand where the numbers came from.

## Checklist Before Shipping

- [ ] Every number has a source and a year
- [ ] No verdict words ("predatory," "insane") in body prose
- [ ] There's a person or scene early (not a statistic)
- [ ] The system is named (pay-to-play, tiered leagues, scholarship scarcity)
- [ ] The consequence is shown (who wins, who loses)
- [ ] Pull quotes carry heat; body prose stays at reporting temperature
- [ ] Ranges, not false precision ($5K–$12K, not $8,437)
- [ ] Every table/chart has a one-line note explaining it
- [ ] If a claim seems damning, is there a counterargument in the text?
- [ ] Can a skeptical journalist fact-check this in an hour?

---

**Why this voice matters:** Youth soccer has absorbed a ton of family money and parental anxiety. A lot of that comes from marketing and hope. Our job is to separate the hope from the reality and let readers see the math. That's only credible if we sound like we're reporting, not preaching.
