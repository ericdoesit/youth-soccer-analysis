Patrick Schulte is the best case in this dataset. Drafted #12 overall in 2022 out of Saint Louis University, he starts in goal for the Columbus Crew at $1,032,083 a year — the only one of 94 surviving 2020–2025 draft picks earning over $1M. He came up through a fee-paying youth club, like the families this site surveys. Everything below him on the salary guide is what the pathway more typically pays.

Roster structure: Each MLS club carries up to 30 players across three tiers. Senior Roster (spots 1–20) counts against the salary budget — these 18–20 players, including up to 3 Designated Players, are the club's real financial commitments. Supplemental spots 21–24 are off-budget but must earn at least the Senior Minimum ($88,025). The DP distortion: 73 Designated Players are 6.6% of the roster but earn 31% of total league payroll — $196M of $631M. The median DP earns $2.6M; the median non-DP earns $304K. When a family imagines their child "making it to MLS," they are almost certainly imagining a DP career. They are statistically projecting into the top 6.6% of an already nearly-impossible outcome.

Where MLS Players Come From

The MLS SuperDraft is 61% American, but the draft is not how MLS builds its rosters. Of the top 100 transfer deals in MLS history, 92.6% of total spend went to non-American players. Argentina accounts for more transfer investment than Mexico, Brazil, and the US combined.

The SuperDraft is dominated by domestic college players — a narrow slice of MLS talent. Stars like Messi (Argentina), Son (South Korea), Lozano (Mexico), and Denkey (Togo) never touched the draft. MLS builds its best squads through the transfer window, not the pipeline that American youth families are funding.

The pipeline reality: American youth families spend $70K+ developing players for a league that fills 59% of its roster spots internationally. The MLS draft is a domestic college pipeline — but the league's actual talent acquisition strategy is global transfers, with South America and Europe as the primary markets. An American kid "making it to MLS" competes against a global pool, not just fellow draft picks.

MLS Career Survival

697 MLS SuperDraft picks across 11 cohorts (2010–2020) tracked against MLSPA salary guides from 2015–2026. The chart below starts at 100% — every player on draft day. Each subsequent point shows what percentage was still collecting an MLS paycheck at that year's September salary guide release.

Being drafted is not the same as being paid. The draft gives a club negotiating rights — it is not a contract. After January's draft, every pick must survive training camp (February–March), be offered a contract, and make the 30-man active roster before the September salary guide is published. The 56% who disappear by that first data point were cut in preseason, never offered a deal, sent to a lower-level affiliate (MLS NEXT Pro, USL), or went abroad. Getting drafted sounds like making it. Statistically, it is the beginning of the next cut.

The MLS NEXT Pipeline Problem

MLS NEXT markets itself as the top developmental league in North America — the direct pathway to professional soccer. Families at independent MLS NEXT clubs pay $5,000–$15,000/year believing they are buying access to that pipeline. The data tells a more complicated story.

MLS Pro Academies (30 clubs)

  • $0 to families — fully funded by MLS clubs
  • MLS clubs spend $50K–$100K+ per player per year in facilities, coaching, and staff
  • Top performers receive Homegrown contracts — bypassing the draft entirely
  • Only 1–3 Homegrown signings per academy per year, but those are guaranteed roster spots
  • Roster spots 29–30 are exclusively Homegrown — structurally reserved, not competitive
  • Players remain MLS club property; overseas interest triggers FIFA training compensation

MLS NEXT Independent Clubs (122 clubs)

  • $5,000–$15,000/year charged to families
  • 5–7 year commitment: $25K–$105K total family investment
  • No Homegrown pathway — players must enter the draft
  • Spots 29–30 are structurally inaccessible — those slots are Homegrown-only by CBA rule
  • Competing against pro academy players whose development was fully subsidized
  • Even R1 picks: only 40% survive 5 years; the average all-pick survival is 22%
The structural problem: The 122 independent MLS NEXT clubs charge families to fund development that feeds the same draft pool the pro academies dominate. The best pro academy players never enter that draft — they already have contracts. And by CBA rule, two roster spots on every MLS team are permanently reserved for pro academy Homegrown players. Independent families are paying premium prices to compete in a pool that has already been skimmed, for roster spots that have been structurally reduced.

Which is why Patrick Schulte matters. He is what winning this lottery looks like: drafted, surviving, a starter, a million-dollar salary. There is exactly one of him in six draft classes. The median survivor earns $113,400 — and the median pick is gone.
