The Public Subsidy Model: LA City United

LA City United

$120 / year (family cost)

LA City United is a competitive club run by LA City Recreation and Parks, funded by LA28 Olympics. Families pay $10/month. The city covers coaches, fields, equipment, insurance, and referees — roughly $1,650/player/year in public subsidy.

What it actually costs to run

LA City United publishes its pay rates publicly. Running one team for one year:

The $120/year family cost is real — but it works because LA28 Olympic funding covers ~93% of the program's operating cost. Coaches earn $23–$31/hour. Referees earn $50/game. These are legitimate labor costs. The public investment makes them invisible to families.

The corrected comparison: Elite youth soccer costs approximately $1,600–$2,000/player/year to deliver efficiently using public infrastructure. Private clubs charge $7,000–$22,000/year — 4–13× the floor cost. The gap funds facility markups, marketing, branding, and profit margins — not meaningfully better coaching. LA City United doesn't prove soccer should be free. It proves families shouldn't bear 100% of the cost.

What Drives the Cost?

Travel costs and tournament fees account for a growing share of total expense as tiers increase — not coaching quality. At Tier 3, families may spend more on travel than on club fees. At Tier 4, national ID camps and private coaching supplement an already high club fee.

The tournament hotel & travel economy

Elite youth soccer tournaments are clustered at soccer-specific facilities that also operate hotels, restaurants, and retail — creating economic incentives for league operators to increase travel requirements rather than reduce them. Families are not just buying soccer development; they are subsidizing a hospitality industry built around their children's games.

The Geography Tax

The same tier of competitive soccer costs 5–6x more in New York City than in Oklahoma City — not because of coaching quality, but because of real estate, coach salaries, and field rental rates. A family in a coastal market pays a structural premium just for geographic exposure.
