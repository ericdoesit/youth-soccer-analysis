The Public Subsidy Model: LA City United

LA City United

$120 / year (all programs, all players)

LA City United is run by LA City Recreation and Parks, funded by LA28 Olympics. Families pay $10/month regardless of whether they are in the rec/prep program or the competitive club program. The city covers coaches, fields, equipment, insurance, and referees. Three program levels operate across four locations (Obama Sports Complex, Whitsett Soccer Fields, Wilmington Athletic Center, Rio de Los Angeles).

Across all ~773 players, the program generates ~$92,800/year in family fees against an estimated ~$1.0M/year in operating costs. Families cover approximately 8% of total program cost. Public funding (LA28 + city) absorbs the other 92%.

The corrected comparison: Competitive youth soccer costs approximately $1,600–$2,000/player/year to deliver using public infrastructure and published labor rates. Rec/prep development costs roughly $430–$550/player/year. Private clubs charge $7,000–$22,000/year for competitive programs — 4–13× the floor cost. The gap funds facility markups, marketing, branding, and profit margins — not meaningfully better coaching. LA City United doesn't prove soccer should be free. It proves families shouldn't bear 100% of the cost.

What Drives the Cost?

Travel costs and tournament fees account for a growing share of total expense as tiers increase — not coaching quality. At Tier 3, families may spend more on travel than on club fees. At Tier 4, national ID camps and private coaching supplement an already high club fee.

The tournament hotel & travel economy

Elite youth soccer tournaments are clustered at soccer-specific facilities that also operate hotels, restaurants, and retail — creating economic incentives for league operators to increase travel requirements rather than reduce them. Families are not just buying soccer development; they are subsidizing a hospitality industry built around their children's games.

The Geography Tax

The same tier of competitive soccer costs 5–6x more in New York City than in Oklahoma City — not because of coaching quality, but because of real estate, coach salaries, and field rental rates. A family in a coastal market pays a structural premium just for geographic exposure.
