The Real Cost of Youth Soccer

From recreational ($468/yr) to elite ($22,000+/yr) — what families actually spend and what they get back.

The biggest number in my survey doesn't belong to the family you'd guess. A San Diego family with a kid on an MLS NEXT pre-academy team pays $3,500 in club fees — and $8,000 a year in private coaching on top of it, six extra hours a week. The club fee is the smallest line on their bill. That is the pattern this page documents: the advertised fee is where the spending starts, not where it ends.

Cost figures on this page are organized by competitive tier (T1–T5). For full tier definitions, leagues, and example clubs see Methodology & Data Sources.

Annual Cost by Tier

Ranges derived from publicly available data: club websites, Aspen Institute Project Play (2024, n=1,848 families), ussoccerparent.com benchmarks, ECNL/MLS NEXT member pages, and verified real-cost data points (One Ivy FC, Albion SC, Girls Academy, Beach FC).

What the fee buys is two things: development and exposure — and the two are tiered differently. A higher fee tier does not reliably buy a higher exposure tier: leagues at the same price point differ enormously in how many scouts ever see a player, and the most scouted environments (MLS pro academies, MLS NEXT Homegrown) are among the cheapest or free. The fee-to-exposure mismatch is where families overpay.

The Public Subsidy Model: LA City United

LA City United

$120 / year (all programs, all players)

LA City United is run by LA City Recreation and Parks, funded by LA28 Olympics. Families pay $10/month regardless of whether they are in the rec/prep program or the competitive club program. The city covers coaches, fields, equipment, insurance, and referees. Three program levels operate across four locations (Obama Sports Complex, Whitsett Soccer Fields, Wilmington Athletic Center, Rio de Los Angeles).

Across all ~773 players, the program generates ~$92,800/year in family fees against an estimated ~$1.0M/year in operating costs. Families cover approximately 8% of total program cost. Public funding (LA28 + city) absorbs the other 92%.

The corrected comparison: Competitive youth soccer costs approximately $1,600–$2,000/player/year to deliver using public infrastructure and published labor rates. Rec/prep development costs roughly $430–$550/player/year. Private clubs charge $7,000–$22,000/year for competitive programs — 4–13× the floor cost. The gap funds facility markups, marketing, branding, and profit margins — not meaningfully better coaching. LA City United doesn't prove soccer should be free. It proves families shouldn't bear 100% of the cost.

What Drives the Cost?

Average annual cost by component — mid estimates, data/costs.csv (n=42 programs)

Travel and tournament fees, not coaching, drive the climb through Tier 3. In the cost database, Tier 3 families average $3,140/yr on travel — more than the $2,910 average club fee itself (39% vs. 36% of total spend). Tier 4 inverts: the club fee dominates at 78% of a ~$23,400 year. Averages of mid estimates across 42 program cost records, data/costs.csv.

The tournament hotel & travel economy

Elite youth soccer tournaments are clustered at soccer-specific facilities that also operate hotels, restaurants, and retail — creating economic incentives for league operators to increase travel requirements rather than reduce them. Families are not just buying soccer development; they are subsidizing a hospitality industry built around their children's games.

The Geography Tax

The same tier of competitive soccer costs 5–6x more in New York City than in Oklahoma City — not because of coaching quality, but because of real estate, coach salaries, and field rental rates. A family in a coastal market pays a structural premium just for geographic exposure.

The driver: field rental. NYC charges $300–500/hour for artificial turf. Oklahoma pays $50–100/hour for the same surface. Coach salaries and cost-of-living amplify the gap. A family in Boise pays Oklahoma City rates — for the same tier of competition, the same coaching credentials, and the same outcomes as a coastal market family paying 5x more.

The San Diego family at the top of this page lives in one of those coastal markets. Six hours a week of private coaching at Southern California rates, on top of an MLS NEXT pre-academy fee. $11,500 a year, and the academy years haven't started.
