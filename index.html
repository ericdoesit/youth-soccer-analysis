The Thesis

The US youth soccer industry generates an estimated $19.8 billion per year by selling families on a pathway — elite club soccer as the route to college scholarships and professional careers. The implicit promise is that higher investment equals better development equals better outcomes.

This research challenges that promise with data. We built a national database of 4,500+ clubs across 15 competitive leagues, combined survey cost data with academic literature, and ran Monte Carlo simulations of expected financial returns by tier. The findings are consistent: elite youth soccer is a negative expected value investment for the vast majority of families — and the families who can least afford to lose are the ones being shut out entirely.

The one exception: LA City United, a publicly funded program that competes at Tier 3–4 level for $120/year. Its existence proves that the premium families pay to private clubs is not for coaching quality — it's for access, status, and the appearance of development advantage.
