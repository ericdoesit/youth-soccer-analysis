The Thesis

The US youth soccer industry generates an estimated $19.8 billion per year by selling families on a pathway — elite club soccer as the route to college scholarships and professional careers. The implicit promise: higher investment → better development → better outcomes.

This research challenges that promise with data. We built a national database of 4,500+ clubs across 15 competitive leagues, combined survey cost data with academic literature, and ran expected-value analysis at each tier. The findings are consistent: elite youth soccer is a negative expected value investment for the vast majority of families — and the families who can least afford to lose are the ones being shut out entirely.

The policy exception: LA City United charges $120/year for Tier 3–4 competitive soccer by absorbing ~$1,647/player/year in public subsidy. Its existence proves that high club fees reflect margin and access, not the inherent cost of the sport.
